## 1. Research overview (self-contained)

### 1.1 One-sentence statement
We compare the **bug patterns** of **AI-generated** and **human-written** *incorrect* solutions to the **same** competitive-programming problems, using a single fixed taxonomy applied identically to both, and we additionally characterize the AI bugs that **persist after autonomous self-reflection**.

### 1.2 Motivation
Students increasingly submit LLM-produced solutions to programming tasks. Prior work has studied AI-generated code bugs and human bugs **separately**, but not **comparatively on the same problems with one taxonomy**. Understanding where AI and human failure modes diverge has direct implications for assessment design, plagiarism/AI-use detection, and tutoring.

### 1.3 Gap and contributions
**Gap.** No study applies a single fixed taxonomy to *both* AI and human buggy submissions on the *same* problem set to characterize how their failure modes diverge.

**Contributions.**
- **C1 — Contamination-controlled paired AI–human bug corpus** built on the CodeContests *test* split (problems published after 2021-09-21), aligned with the September-2021 knowledge cutoff of `gpt-3.5-turbo`. This guarantees (a) the AI solver could not have memorized the problems, and (b) every human submission predates the public release of ChatGPT (2022-11-30), eliminating reverse-contamination from humans covertly using AI.
- **C2 — Automated, reusable classification pipeline** that operationalizes an existing *manual* taxonomy into a scalable, provenance-blind instrument (deterministic verdict-based primary stage + execution-grounded LLM-as-judge secondary stage), validated against a human-labeled gold subset.
- **C3 — Comparative analysis** of AI vs. human bug-pattern distributions, stratified by difficulty and algorithm type, testing the "same categories, different frequencies" hypothesis and isolating arm-distinctive failure modes.
- **C4 — Characterization of persistent (self-irreparable) AI failures**: using autonomous self-reflection purely as a diagnostic probe (not an improvement method) to isolate bug classes that survive N rounds of feedback-driven self-repair.
- **C5 — Replication package** (corpus, prompts, trajectories, pipeline, gold labels, analysis scripts).

### 1.4 Research questions
- **RQ1 (distribution).** On the same problems, how do bug-pattern distributions differ between human and zero-shot-AI incorrect submissions?
- **RQ2 (distinctiveness).** Controlling for difficulty and algorithm type, which patterns are AI-distinctive and which are human-distinctive?
- **RQ3 (persistence).** Which bug classes persist after autonomous self-reflection, i.e., constitute the AI's self-irreparable failure modes?
- **RQ4 (pipeline validity).** How reliably does the automated pipeline reproduce human expert labels?

### 1.5 Closest prior work and how we differ
The closest prior study (Wei et al., *Evaluating and Improving LLM-based Competitive Program Generation*, Inf. & Softw. Tech., 2025) builds a hierarchical error taxonomy for LLM-generated competitive programs and proposes an *improvement* framework. **It analyzes AI only — no human baseline, no comparison.** We **reuse their taxonomy as our measurement instrument** (we do **not** claim taxonomy construction as a contribution) and add the comparative AI-vs-human axis and the persistence analysis. Cite Wei et al. as the taxonomy source and the nearest prior work; state the gap explicitly.

> **Guardrail.** Do not invent a new taxonomy. Do not frame self-reflection as "improving accept rate." Frame it as *characterizing what cannot be autonomously fixed*.

## 2. Pre-registered design decisions (FIXED — do not change without human sign-off)

### 2.1 Dataset and problem set
- **Dataset:** Google DeepMind **CodeContests** (Hugging Face: `deepmind/code_contests`).
- **Problem set P:** the **test split** only. Rationale: CodeContests uses a strict temporal split where test problems were published after **2021-09-21**, which is after the `gpt-3.5-turbo` knowledge cutoff (September 2021). Thus the AI solver cannot have memorized these problems, and the human submissions in the dataset all predate ChatGPT.
- **Borderline/sensitivity set:** the **validation split** (problems 2021-07-15 … 2021-09-20) falls *before* the `gpt-3.5-turbo` cutoff, so those problems may be in the model's training data. Use it **only** as a **contamination probe** — a sensitivity analysis asking whether AI bug distributions differ on likely-seen (validation) vs. unseen (test) problems. If distributions are similar, contamination is unlikely to drive the main results; if they differ, report it as a caveat. Never merge it into the main test-split comparison.
- **Human arm source:** the `incorrect_solutions` field of each test problem (these are real human rejected submissions).
- **Stratification metadata:** `cf_rating` (difficulty) and `cf_tags` (algorithm types). Retain `difficulty` and `source`. The analysis language is fixed to **C++** (Section 2.2), so language is not a stratification variable; non-C++ submissions are kept only for the descriptive language-distribution report.

### 2.2 AI generation model (FIXED — contamination-critical)
- **Generator model:** `gpt-3.5-turbo` (use a pinned snapshot, e.g. `gpt-3.5-turbo-0125`, recorded in `DECISIONS.md`). Rationale: it is the GPT-family model whose September-2021 cutoff matches the test-split boundary while predating ChatGPT-era public AI use. **Do not** substitute a newer model — that would break the contamination alignment that the entire human-arm cleanliness depends on.
- **HARD DEADLINE (scheduling).** OpenAI has scheduled `gpt-3.5-turbo` (incl. snapshots and fine-tunes) for **API shutdown on 2026-10-23**. All generator calls (Phase D: zero-shot + self-reflection) **must complete before that date**. Plan the run accordingly; the cache (Section 3.4) preserves results so later phases (judging, analysis, paper) can run after the shutdown. If the date is near, prioritize Phase D. (Re-confirm the date on OpenAI's deprecations page at run time.)
- **STOP-and-ask:** If `gpt-3.5-turbo` is unavailable at run time, halt and ask the human. Do not silently switch models. (Document the availability check in `RUN_LOG.md`.)
- **Decoding:** temperature **1.0**, top_p default; record exact params. Justification: higher temperature maximizes diversity, which is what we want — a representative spread of bugs, not one canonical attempt.
- **Samples per problem (zero-shot) — collect-until-enough, capped:** for each problem, generate samples until **≥ 10 non-AC (buggy) submissions** are collected, but stop at a **hard cap of k_max = 15–20 attempts** (set the exact `k_max` in `config.yaml`). Rationale: on competitive problems gpt-3.5-turbo's pass rate is low, so most attempts are buggy; this rule yields up to ~10 bugs per problem (a balanced corpus) while not wasting calls on problems the model happens to solve. Record per problem how many attempts were made and how many non-AC were kept. **Mirror this on the human side:** cap retained human incorrect submissions per problem at the same number (~10) for symmetry (Section 2.7 clustering still applies). Verify sufficiency empirically via the saturation curve (Phase G); if new leaf categories are still appearing, raise the cap and document it.
- **Language (FIXED — C++ only):** the study analyzes **C++ only**. The human arm, AI generation, judging, classification, and analysis all use C++; **Python3 is not generated and is excluded from the analysis pipeline.** Rationale: parts of the reused Wei et al. taxonomy are language-dependent (e.g., GE4 integer-overflow/precision leaves apply to C++ but are essentially unreachable in Python3's arbitrary-precision integers; slow unsynchronized-I/O and compile-error handling also differ by language), so a single C++ arm maximizes taxonomy fit and removes language as a confound. C++ is also the competitive-programming standard and by far the largest arm — collected human non-AC counts are **17,670 C++ vs 7,273 Python3** (Java 8,463, Python2 166), and C++ coverage is uniform across difficulty bins whereas Python3 is sparse on hard/expert problems. `primary_language` is fixed to `cpp` in `config.yaml`; there is **no** post-collection language-selection step. The already-collected Python3/Java/Python2 human counts are **kept for reporting only** (a language-distribution descriptive statistic in the paper) and are **never** fed into the analysis pipeline.

### 2.3 Self-reflection protocol (for RQ3)
- **Loop:** up to **N = 5** iterations of autonomous self-repair. No human-engineered repair strategies (that is what distinguishes us from Wei et al.).
- **Feedback granularity (FIXED):** after each failed attempt, return to the model: (i) the **verdict** (WA/RE/TLE/CE), and (ii) **one failing test case** as `(input, expected_output, actual_output)` — chosen as the first failing case in canonical test order. This mirrors what a student would realistically paste back. Do **not** feed the oracle solution to the generator. Record the full trajectory.
- **Stopping:** stop early if AC is reached. The **residual set** for RQ3 = submissions still incorrect after iteration N.

### 2.4 Taxonomy (FIXED — reused instrument)
Use the **Wei et al. (2025) hierarchical taxonomy** verbatim. Two top groups: **General Errors (GE)** and **Algorithm-specific Errors (AE)**. Reproduce the full leaf list from Appendix A of this document. Do **not** add, rename, or merge categories except via the controlled "uncovered category" procedure in Phase E.5, which requires logging and human notification.

> **Code caveat.** The `GE*/AE*` codes used throughout this document (in the Phase E mapping and examples) mirror Wei et al.'s structure but are **placeholders**. Your first step is to retrieve the authors' actual taxonomy (codes, leaves, examples) from their public artifact and pin it; if the real codes or granularity differ, **update every cross-reference in this plan consistently** (the verdict→candidate-set mapping, the repair-patch examples, the schemas) so the code set is internally coherent.
>
> **Construct validity & language dependence (why C++ is primary).** Several taxonomy leaves are language-dependent: GE4 (overflow / precision from improper type selection) is essentially C++-specific and unreachable in Python3's arbitrary-precision integers; GE5.1 (compilation errors) and GE5.2 (language-specific syntax/feature misuse, e.g. slow unsynchronized I/O) manifest differently across languages. We therefore fix the analysis language to **C++** (Section 2.2) so the reused taxonomy applies cleanly and language is removed as a confound. When you retrieve and pin the authors' actual taxonomy from their public repository (`github.com/minnanWei/LLMs-Competitive-Program-Generation`), record for each leaf its canonical example **and its example language**, and compile the explicit list of **language-dependent leaves** into `DECISIONS.md`; use that list when interpreting which leaves are meaningful for the C++ arm.

### 2.5 Verdict scheme (FIXED)
Primary verdicts from execution: **AC, WA, RE, TLE, CE** (Accepted, Wrong Answer, Runtime Error, Time Limit Exceeded, Compile Error). Definitions in Appendix B.

### 2.6 Judge/classifier model (selected empirically, NOT contamination-critical)
- **The judge is a *labeler with execution grounding*, not a problem solver,** so its training cutoff is irrelevant. Critically, our judge does **not** decide correctness (execution/verdict already does that); it only assigns a taxonomy *category* to an already-confirmed bug, given the verdict, the first failing test, the **oracle** solution, and the **verified repair patch** (E.3). This sidesteps the known weakness that LLMs misjudge code *correctness* — prior work (Crupi et al., TSE 2025) shows even the best judge mis-rates correctness, but that is a different task from grounded categorization.
- **Model choice is made empirically, not asserted.** Following SE LLM-as-judge practice, evaluate a **slate of two frontier judges** against the human gold set (Phase F), report each judge's **κ and macro-F1** vs. the human consensus, and **select the best-aligned as the production judge.** The slate (defaults as of mid-2026; pin the exact snapshots in `config.yaml`/`DECISIONS.md` and use the latest available at run time, since the judge is not contamination-critical):
  - **OpenAI GPT-5-class** — the current strongest GPT-5.x production snapshot (e.g. `gpt-5.5`); confirm the exact model string on OpenAI's models page at run time.
  - **Anthropic Claude-Sonnet-class** — `claude-sonnet-4-6` (GA, supports structured output), or the latest Sonnet snapshot available at run time.
  Both must support **structured/JSON output** (so labels parse reliably). Disclose the slate, the per-model agreement, and the chosen model in the paper. This makes the judge-model decision a *measured* result (exactly what RQ4 quantifies), not an arbitrary one.
- **Bias controls (FIXED):** judge prompts are **provenance-blind** (no arm identifier, no origin-encoding id/filename); use **self-consistency** (sample the judge **m times**, default **m = 5**, configurable down to **3** to cut cost; majority-vote per category). Because the judge model differs from the generator (`gpt-3.5-turbo`) and labels are single-item (not pairwise), self-preference and position biases do not apply. (m = number of judge queries per submission; higher m = more stable, proportionally more cost.)

### 2.7 Statistical plan (FIXED at the level of methods)
- Primary distribution comparison (RQ1): per-category counts, two-proportion tests, **chi-square**/Fisher across the category vector, with **Benjamini–Hochberg** FDR correction for multiple categories.
- Effect sizes: **Cramér's V** (overall), **Cohen's h** (per-category proportion differences). Always report effect sizes alongside p-values.
- Stratified analysis (RQ2): control for difficulty (`cf_rating` bins) and algorithm type (`cf_tags`) via **Cochran–Mantel–Haenszel** and/or logistic regression (`bug_category ~ arm + difficulty + type`). Report adjusted associations.
- Persistence (RQ3): same pipeline on the residual set; compare the residual leaf distribution to the self-reflection arm's **own turn-0 (initial-attempt)** distribution; report which leaves shrink vs. persist.
- Reliability (RQ4): inter-annotator **Cohen's κ** (humans); pipeline-vs-human per-class **precision/recall/F1** and **macro-F1**.
- **Non-independence (FIXED — submissions are clustered by problem).** Multiple human submissions and the k AI samples share a problem, so they are **not** independent; naive chi-square/proportion tests overstate significance. Account for clustering: report a **problem-clustered** analysis as primary — e.g., mixed-effects logistic regression with a random intercept per problem (`leaf_present ~ arm + difficulty + type + (1|problem)`), or cluster-robust standard errors, or a per-problem-aggregated analysis. The unadjusted tests may be shown for reference but the clustered result is the one interpreted. State this in Threats to Validity (conclusion validity).
- Significance level α = 0.05 (post-correction). Pre-register these in the paper's methods.

### 2.8 Compute budget and scale
This pipeline is LLM-call-heavy. Rough order of magnitude (≈165 problems, **C++ only**): zero-shot generation up to problems × k_max; self-reflection up to problems × (1 + N); Tier-2 judging ≈ (all C++ non-AC submissions across **both** arms — human and AI) × m; plus repair calls for WA cases. Per the user's decision, **there is no budget ceiling — do not stop on cost** (`max_usd` is disabled/null). Still:
- **Estimate and log cost.** Before each LLM-heavy phase, compute the projected call/token count and estimated cost and write it to `RUN_LOG.md` (for transparency and the paper's cost reporting) — just do not halt on it.
- The cache (Section 3.4) must make re-runs free; never pay twice for the same prompt.
- If you ever need to reduce volume, the **only** sanctioned reduction is to lower per-problem submission **caps symmetrically** for both arms (and/or reduce `m`), recorded in `DECISIONS.md` — never reduce one arm only (breaks symmetry), and never silently drop the contamination/language constraints.
- Order operations to fail cheap: run Phase 0/A/B and a **small pilot** (e.g., 5 problems end-to-end through Phase G) before the full run, to surface bugs early.

### 2.9 Final dataset composition (FIXED — C++ single arm)

This is the authoritative definition of what enters the analysis; the rules are operationalized in Phase A (problem + human filtering), at the end of Phase D (AI generation → intersection), and in Phase G (aggregation, sparse categories, sensitivity).

> **Final dataset = { problems with (human C++ non-AC ≥ 1) **AND** (AI C++ non-AC ≥ 1) }.** Report the intersection problem count and the per-arm total retained submissions.

1. **Problem-set start.** CodeContests **test** split ∩ **Codeforces** source (has `cf_rating`/`cf_tags`) ∩ published **post-2021-09-21**.
2. **Human-arm filter.** Of each problem's `incorrect_solutions`, keep **C++ only**, and keep only those whose **harness re-judged verdict is non-AC**; submissions that unexpectedly judge AC are dropped and counted.
3. **AI-arm generation.** For the same problems, generate **C++** with `gpt-3.5-turbo`, collecting until **10 non-AC** per problem (hard cap `k_max` = 15–20).
4. **Per-problem inclusion floor.** Include a problem if it has **≥ 1 non-AC** (no high per-problem floor); problems with 0 non-AC are excluded (in C++ this is only 1 of the 165 on the human side).
5. **Both-arm intersection.** The **primary** RQ1/RQ2 analysis runs on problems with **both** human and AI C++ non-AC ≥ 1 (the intersection). Problems present in only one arm are reported as secondary/descriptive only. Always report the intersection problem count and per-arm submission totals.
6. **Symmetric cap.** Both arms use the **same** per-problem cap (~10). When an arm exceeds the cap for a problem, draw a **uniform random** sample within that problem×arm (record the seed). Do **not** force a 1:1 per-problem count match — the counts are themselves data, and any imbalance is absorbed by the problem-clustered model (Section 2.7).
7. **Sparse-category handling.** For chi-square validity, when a tested category's expected frequency is insufficient (rule of thumb < 5), **collapse** that leaf into its parent family or report it descriptively only.
8. **Sensitivity (matched) analysis.** The main analysis is full-corpus + clustering; an **appendix** adds a **1:1 matched** version (equal random draw per arm per problem) to preempt the "count imbalance is unfair" objection.

## 3. Repository and environment setup (Phase 0)

### 3.0 Code style & conventions (MANDATORY — applies to `run.py` and everything in `src/`)
Consistency here matters more than cleverness; the repo must stay uniform so it is maintainable after the study. Follow every rule:

1. **Single entry point.** `run.py` lives at the repo root and is the only executable. Everything runs through its subcommands/options (use `argparse` subcommands). No other file is run directly. All non-entry code lives under `src/`.
2. **One step = one file = one class.** Each process/step is a single file containing exactly **one class**; all of that step's functionality is implemented as **methods** of that class. No free functions at module level (helpers are private methods, e.g. `_helper`).
3. **Naming.** File name in `snake_case`, class name in `CamelCase`, and the two must correspond: `problem_set_builder.py` ↔ `ProblemSetBuilder`.
4. **Shared code** goes in `src/common/` (config, schemas, LLM client) or `src/utils/` (generic helpers). Same one-class-per-file rule applies.
5. **Packages.** Every folder under `src/` has an `__init__.py` that re-exports its class(es) as `from .file_name import ClassName`. Do **not** define `__all__`.
6. **Minimalism (experimental repo).** Implement only what the study needs. No defensive/excessive `try/except`, no configuration frameworks, no plugin systems, no speculative abstractions, no unused parameters. If an error should stop the run, let it raise. (The *only* exception is the LLM client's retry/cache and the sandbox's resource limits, which are part of the method's job.)
7. **No noise imports.** Do not add `from __future__ import annotations` or any import that isn't actually used.
8. **No docstrings.** Do not write `"""..."""` docstrings anywhere.
9. **Comments.** Use `#` comments only, sparingly — at the start of a step/process block or on a genuinely non-obvious line. No comment restating what the code already says.
10. **Mirrored tests.** Every step file has a matching test in `tests/` at the same relative path/filename (Section 3.0.1). Write each test right after its step and make it pass before moving on — implement, test, debug, then proceed.

## 4. Phase A — Problem-set construction

**Objective.** Produce the fixed problem set P and per-problem metadata.

**Steps.**
1. Load `deepmind/code_contests`. Cache to `data/raw/`.
2. Select the **test** split as P. (Also stash the validation split separately for the sensitivity analysis; do not mix.)
3. For each problem, extract and store to `data/problems/`: `problem_id`, `name`, `description`, all test cases (`public_tests`, `private_tests`, `generated_tests`), `time_limit`, `memory_limit`, `cf_rating`, `cf_tags`, `difficulty`, `source`, the set of **correct** `solutions` (oracle, by language), and the set of `incorrect_solutions` (human arm raw). The **analysis arm is C++ only** (Section 2.2): the C++ `incorrect_solutions` are the human arm; Python3/Java/Python2 `incorrect_solutions` are retained **for the descriptive language-distribution report only** and never enter the pipeline.
4. Compute and save a **problem manifest** (`data/problems/manifest.csv`): id, rating, tags, #public/#private/#generated tests, #correct solutions, and #incorrect human solutions per language (the per-language counts feed the descriptive language-distribution report; the **C++ count is the analysis-arm size**).
5. Bin difficulty (`cf_rating`) into a small number of ordered bins; record bin edges in `DECISIONS.md`. Normalize `cf_tags` into your analysis algorithm-type set (map to Wei et al. AE families where possible).
6. **Source/metadata reality (important for RQ2).** CodeContests aggregates multiple judges (`source`: Codeforces, AtCoder, CodeChef, Aizu, …); `cf_rating` and `cf_tags` are populated **only for Codeforces-sourced problems**. The contamination window (post-2021-09-21) holds by date across all sources, but algorithm-type/difficulty stratification (RQ2) needs that metadata. Default: **restrict the main study to Codeforces-sourced test problems** (cleanest dates + full metadata), and report the count. If you keep non-Codeforces problems, treat "no tag/rating" as its own stratum and state the limitation. Record the choice in `DECISIONS.md`.
7. **Apply the final-dataset rules (Section 2.9).** This phase realizes rules 1 (problem-set start: test ∩ Codeforces ∩ post-2021-09-21) and prepares the human arm for rules 2 and 4: the **C++ non-AC** human submissions (re-judged in Phase C) with the per-problem inclusion floor of **≥ 1 non-AC**. The AI side and the both-arm intersection are finalized at the end of Phase D.

**Deliverables.** `data/problems/*`, `manifest.csv`.

**Validation gate.** Manifest row count == #test problems; every problem has ≥1 oracle solution and ≥1 test case; report how many test problems contain **C++** `incorrect_solutions` (the analysis arm depends on this — if surprisingly low, flag in `ASSUMPTIONS.md`); also report the per-language incorrect counts for the descriptive report.

**Stop-and-ask.** If the number of test problems with usable **C++** human incorrect solutions is too small for statistical power (e.g., the per-arm corpus would be tiny), pause and present options (e.g., supplementing by scraping additional 2021-09…2022-11 Codeforces submissions with verdicts) to the human before proceeding.

## 5. Phase B — Sandboxed judge/execution harness

**Objective.** A safe, deterministic harness that compiles/runs untrusted code against CodeContests tests and returns a verdict **plus rich execution signals**.

> Submissions (human and AI) are untrusted code. Execute **only** inside a locked-down sandbox.

**Steps.**
1. Build a Docker image with the needed toolchain: **C++ (g++, matching a standard competitive setup)** — the only language judged in the analysis pipeline. Pin the compiler version. (A Python3 interpreter is only needed if you re-judge non-C++ submissions for the descriptive language report; it is not part of the analysis path.)
2. Sandbox constraints per run: **no network**, read-only FS except a scratch dir, CPU/wall-time limit = problem `time_limit` (+ small margin), memory limit = problem `memory_limit`, output size cap, non-root user.
3. Implement `run_submission(problem, source, language) -> ExecutionResult` where `ExecutionResult` includes: `verdict ∈ {AC,WA,RE,TLE,CE}`, the **first failing test** `(input, expected, actual)`, compiler stderr (if CE), runtime exception/stack and exit signal (if RE), peak time & memory, and per-test pass/fail vector. (See schema Appendix C.)
4. Verdict precedence: CE > RE > TLE > WA > AC (i.e., if it doesn't compile it's CE; etc.). Define output comparison (trailing-whitespace/newline normalization) and record the exact comparison rule — competitive judges are strict; match CodeContests conventions and document them.
5. Make runs **deterministic and cached** by `(problem_id, hash(source), language)`.

**Deliverables.** The `judge` package (sandbox runner + verdict logic) plus a `run.py check-judge` subcommand that judges single and batch submissions and runs the oracle-AC self-test.

**Validation gate.** Sanity tests: every problem's **oracle** solutions must judge **AC** under your harness. If an oracle solution is not AC, your harness or comparison rule is wrong — fix before proceeding. Report oracle-AC rate (should be ~100%; investigate any miss). Confirm sandbox blocks network and enforces limits (include a deliberately offending test program).

## 6. Phase C — Human-arm corpus (Tier 0 for humans)

**Objective.** A clean set of human **incorrect** submissions with verdicts and execution signals.

**Steps.**
1. For each problem in P, take its **C++** `incorrect_solutions` (the analysis arm; Section 2.2). Pre-filter obvious noise only: empty/near-empty files, wrong-language entries, or fragments that are clearly not a real attempt (log counts removed and why). **Do not** filter genuine compile errors — a real submission that fails to compile is a legitimate bug (verdict CE → a syntax leaf), so it stays. When unsure, keep it and let the harness assign the verdict.
2. Judge each with the Phase B harness. **Keep only submissions whose harness verdict is non-AC** (genuinely incorrect under our oracle; Section 2.9 rule 2) — this is the noise filter the study relies on. Submissions that unexpectedly judge AC are dropped (and counted).
3. Persist normalized records to `artifacts/human_submissions/` and their `ExecutionResult` to `artifacts/executions/`. Schema in Appendix C. Enrich each retained record with the judge-derived `verdict`, `peak_time_ms`, and `peak_mem_kb` (CodeContests provides only code; these come from our harness).
4. **Cap per problem at ~10** retained C++ human incorrect submissions to mirror the AI side (Section 2.2 / 2.9 rule 6) and stop a few high-traffic problems from dominating; if over the cap, sample uniformly at random within that problem (record the seed). Apply the **≥ 1 non-AC** per-problem floor (Section 2.9 rule 4). Record the cap in `DECISIONS.md`.

**Deliverables.** Normalized C++ human incorrect corpus + executions + a per-problem count table (plus the descriptive per-language incorrect counts from Phase A for the language-distribution report).

**Validation gate.** All retained human submissions are non-AC under the harness; per-problem cap (~10) respected; the ≥1 non-AC floor applied; counts logged. No AC leakage into the "incorrect" set.

## 7. Phase D — AI-arm generation (zero-shot + self-reflection)

**Objective.** Produce AI incorrect submissions for the same problems, in two conditions.

**Steps.**
1. **Availability check:** confirm the pinned `gpt-3.5-turbo` snapshot responds; record. If not, STOP-and-ask (Section 2.2). Note the 2026-10-23 shutdown deadline — finish all generation before then.
2. **Zero-shot (C++):** for each problem, generate **C++** with the Appendix D.1 prompt (temperature **1.0**), extracting the code block robustly and judging each attempt, **until ≥ 10 non-AC submissions are collected for that problem, capped at k_max = 15–20 attempts** (Section 2.2 / 2.9 rule 3; exact `k_max` from `config.yaml`). Retain the non-AC samples as the AI zero-shot corpus; keep AC ones separately for pass-rate reporting (they are not bugs). If a problem yields fewer than 10 non-AC within k_max, keep what you got and record the shortfall. A sample with no extractable code is a non-response → drop it and count it (do not treat it as CE).
3. **Self-reflection (RQ3):** start from one initial C++ attempt per problem (Appendix D.2 prompt); if non-AC, run up to **N = 5** repair turns, each feeding back verdict + one failing test (Section 2.3, Appendix D.3). Stop on AC. Persist the **entire trajectory** (every turn's prompt, code, verdict, signals). The **residual set** = trajectories still non-AC after turn N.
4. Log generation provenance (arm, model, params) separately from the code so it can be stripped before judging (provenance-blind requirement). Language is fixed to C++.
5. **Finalize the analysis dataset (Section 2.9).** With both arms collected, compute the **both-arm intersection**: problems with (human C++ non-AC ≥ 1) **AND** (AI C++ non-AC ≥ 1) — this is the primary RQ1/RQ2 set (rule 5). Apply the symmetric per-problem cap (~10) with uniform random sampling over the cap (record the seed; rule 6). Report the intersection problem count and per-arm total retained submissions. Problems present in only one arm are kept as secondary/descriptive only. (No language-selection step — the language is fixed to C++.)

**Deliverables.** `artifacts/ai_submissions/zero_shot/*`, `artifacts/ai_submissions/self_reflection/*` (full trajectories), executions, a pass-rate report (AC rate, AC after each reflection turn), and the finalized intersection set with per-arm counts.

**Validation gate.** Each problem has up to ~10 retained non-AC samples (or a recorded shortfall) within k_max; self-reflection trajectories are well-formed (monotone turn count ≤ N, stop-on-AC respected); residual set identified; the both-arm intersection is computed and its problem count + per-arm submission totals reported. Provenance is stored **outside** the code payload.

## 8. Phase E — Classification pipeline (Tiers 1–2)

> Applied **identically** to human and AI arms. Input: any non-AC submission + its `ExecutionResult` + the problem (description, oracle solution available to the judge only).

> **Execution order (important).** The Tier-2 judge model is **selected empirically in Phase F** (slate evaluated on the human gold set). So run the gold labeling + judge selection (Phase F.1 and F.2 steps 1–5) **before** Tier-2 labeling of the full corpus here, and use the selected model. Tier 1 (deterministic, no model) may run anytime. Sequence: build corpora (C/D) → sample + human-label gold + select judge (F) → label the full corpus with the chosen judge (E) → finish RQ4 scoring (already produced in F) → analysis (G). This order is also cost-optimal: you never run the expensive full-corpus labeling with an unvalidated judge.

**E.1 Tier 1 — deterministic primary labeling.** From the verdict, assign a **candidate set** of taxonomy leaves (not a final leaf). Mapping (record exact mapping in code, summary here):
- **CE →** GE5 (syntax) candidates.
- **TLE →** {GE1.3 inefficient design, GE5.2 language-specific slow I/O, AE5.4 unexploited overlap, AE6.* search blowups} candidates.
- **RE →** {GE2 boundary, GE4 overflow-induced} candidates.
- **WA →** all semantic candidates {GE1, GE2, GE3, GE6, AE*}.
This tier is free, deterministic, and already yields the **primary distribution** (CE/RE/TLE/WA proportions) for each arm.

**E.2 Tier 2 — execution-grounded LLM-as-judge (secondary).** For each non-AC submission, call the judge (provenance-blind) with: problem description, the buggy code, the verdict, the first failing test `(input, expected, actual)`, compiler/runtime messages, the **oracle** solution (as reference), the **candidate set** from Tier 1, and the taxonomy rubric (Appendix A, with one canonical example per leaf). Require **structured JSON, multi-label**, constrained to taxonomy enums and (preferably) to the candidate set. Sample **m = 5** times; majority-vote per leaf. (Appendix D.4 prompt.)

**E.3 Repair-and-diff grounding (strengthens Tier 2).** For WA/semantic cases, ask a separate *repair* call to produce a **minimal patch** that turns the buggy code into an AC solution (verified by the harness, using the oracle only as reference). Characterize the patch (e.g., `int→long long` ⇒ GE4.1; added `visited[]` ⇒ AE3.1; `<`→`<=` ⇒ GE2.2). Feed the verified patch description into the final label decision so labels are **grounded in an executable change**, not the judge's unverified opinion. Cap repair attempts; if no verified minimal patch is found, fall back to judge-only label and flag lower confidence.

**E.4 Multi-label policy.** A submission may carry several leaves (Wei et al. allow this). Record all; for distribution analysis use a clearly documented counting rule (e.g., count each leaf once per submission). Keep raw multi-label records for sensitivity.

**E.5 Uncovered-category procedure (controlled).** If the judge cannot fit a bug, label it `UNCOVERED` with a free-text description. Do **not** create new categories on the fly. After the run, aggregate `UNCOVERED` cases; if a coherent recurring pattern emerges, log it in `ASSUMPTIONS.md` and **notify the human** — adding a category is a human-approved decision, reported transparently in the paper.

**Deliverables.** `artifacts/labels/primary/*`, `artifacts/labels/secondary/*`, patch records, judge call logs.

**Validation gate.** No CE submission receives a non-syntax-only label that contradicts compilation; label JSON validates against the taxonomy enum; m-sample majority recorded with agreement stats; provenance never present in any judge prompt (assert in code).

## 9. Phase F — Validation & reliability (Tier 3) — HUMAN-IN-THE-LOOP

**Objective.** Establish label trustworthiness and **select the production judge** by measuring how well candidate judges reproduce human expert labels (RQ4). This is the **only** phase requiring the human to do real labeling. It runs **before** the full-corpus Tier-2 labeling (Phase E) — see the execution-order note in Phase E. The agent makes labeling as easy as possible (ready-to-fill CSVs in `human/`), then **pauses**; the human fills the files; the human signals completion; the agent **resumes** automatically to select the judge and continue.

### F.1 — Prepare the human-labeling packet (automated; ends in a STOP)
Run via `run.py prepare-gold` (→ `GoldSampler`).
1. **Sample a gold subset, sized by an accepted statistical rule (not an arbitrary count).** Determine the gold-set size with the **Cochran sample-size formula for a proportion** (binomial proportion CI), at **95% confidence, margin e = ±5%, p = 0.5** (most conservative), then apply the **finite-population correction** to the actual non-AC population. This yields ≈ **385** items for a large population (smaller after FPC); record the exact computed n and inputs in `DECISIONS.md`. This matches the established practice in SE LLM-as-judge studies, which size human-validation sets this way (typically a few hundred; e.g., ~95/stratum at ±10%, ~370–390 total at ±5%). **Stratify** the sample across **arm × verdict** (single language, C++) with equal or proportional allocation so every cell is represented. If reviewers/you want per-arm guarantees, size each arm at 95%/±5% (≈ 385 each) instead and note it. Assign each item an **opaque, shuffled `item_id`** (e.g. `g0001`) that does not encode arm or order.
   - *Margin is the only lever:* ±5% → ≈385 (rigorous, default); ±10% → ≈95/stratum (lighter). Pick the margin in `config.yaml`; the formula fixes the rest.
2. **Write the provenance keymap** `artifacts/gold/keymap.json` mapping `item_id → {submission_id, arm}`. This file is **git-ignored and never shown to the human** — it preserves blinding.
3. **Write a human-readable detail file per item** at `human/items/<item_id>.md` containing, with NO arm/provenance and NO model-predicted labels: the (size-capped) problem description, the buggy code, the verdict, and the first failing test `(input, expected, actual)`. The human opens this to understand the item.
4. **Write the codebook** `human/CODEBOOK.md`: every taxonomy code (`GE*/AE*`) with its name and one canonical example, taken from the retrieved Wei et al. taxonomy. This is the labeling reference.
5. **Write two independent annotator CSVs** with the **same** item set, both provenance-blind: `human/annotation_annotator1.csv` and `human/annotation_annotator2.csv`. Columns (see Appendix C for the exact spec): read-only `item_id`, `verdict`, `detail_file`; fill-in `labels`, `uncovered`, `confidence`, `notes`. Leave the fill-in columns empty.
   - **No label hints in the packet.** Do not include any model-derived or verdict-derived candidate codes — annotators read the codebook and decide independently. (Including hints, even deterministic ones, risks anchoring the human to the pipeline and weakens the gold standard's independence.) The LLM judge's predicted labels must, of course, never appear in `human/`.
6. **Write `human/README.md`**: plain instructions — how to open a `detail_file`, how to enter one or more codes in `labels` separated by `;` (e.g. `GE1.2;GE2.1`), when to set `uncovered = yes`, that `confidence`/`notes` are optional, that annotator 1 and annotator 2 must be **two different people working independently** (do not copy each other), and how to signal completion (tell the agent "annotation complete").
7. **STOP-and-ask:** print exactly which files the human must fill (`human/annotation_annotator1.csv`, `human/annotation_annotator2.csv`) and wait. Do not proceed until the human says the two files are complete.

### F.2 — Ingest, adjudicate, and score (automated; resumes on the human's signal)
Run via `run.py score-reliability` (→ `ReliabilityScorer`) **after** the human says the two annotator files are done.
1. **Validate the filled CSVs.** Every row has either ≥1 valid taxonomy code in `labels` or `uncovered = yes`; codes are valid enum values; no blank/garbled rows; item sets match between the two files. If anything is wrong, **report the exact offending `item_id`s back to the human and stop** — do not guess or auto-fix.
2. **Inter-annotator agreement.** Compute **Cohen's κ** between annotator 1 and annotator 2 (multi-label: report per-label κ and a summary; target ≥ 0.7, report regardless).
3. **Adjudication (third person).** Compute the disagreements and write `human/annotation_adjudication.csv` containing **only the disputed items**, showing both annotators' labels read-only plus an empty `final_labels` column. **STOP-and-ask** for the third person (adjudicator) to fill `final_labels`, then signal completion. (If there are zero disagreements, skip this step and note it.)
4. **Build the human consensus gold labels** = agreed labels where the two annotators match, plus the adjudicator's `final_labels` for disputes. Persist to `artifacts/gold/consensus_labels.json`.
5. **Score the judge slate and select the production judge.** Run each candidate judge model (Section 2.6) over the gold items and compute, per model, **per-class precision/recall/F1, macro-F1, and Cohen's κ** vs. the human consensus; **select the best-aligned model** as the production judge (used to label the full corpus in Phase E). Report the full slate comparison and the choice in the paper. Join back to provenance via `keymap.json` only here (server-side), never in the human packet.
6. For any class with low F1, mark pipeline labels of that class as **low-confidence**; Phase G must report results with and without low-confidence classes (sensitivity).

> *Documented fallback (only with explicit human authorization):* if no human annotators are available at all, a panel of **≥3 strong, diverse LLMs** may produce "silver" labels with disagreement flagged, **clearly labeled in the paper as a weaker substitute** with its validity caveats. Do not present silver labels as gold.

**Deliverables.** `human/` packet (detail files, codebook, README, the three CSVs), `artifacts/gold/{keymap.json, consensus_labels.json}`, `results/stats/reliability.*` (κ, P/R/F1, confusion matrix).

**Validation gate.** Packet generated and provenance-blind (no arm leakage, no judge labels in `human/`); filled CSVs validated; κ computed; adjudication resolved; pipeline P/R/F1 and macro-F1 reported; low-F1 classes flagged.

> **Why this protects the study even if the judge is imperfect:** because the *same* pipeline is applied to both arms, a systematic judge bias acts *symmetrically* and largely cancels in the *difference* between arms. RQ4 quantifies the residual risk.

> **Handoff summary (what the human actually does):** open `human/README.md` → for each row, read its `detail_file` and enter codes in `labels` (and `uncovered`/`notes` as needed) in **both** annotator files (two different people) → say "annotation complete" → if asked, fill `final_labels` in `human/annotation_adjudication.csv` for the few disputed items → say "complete". The agent does everything else.

## 10. Phase G — Analysis (Tier 4)

**Objective.** Answer RQ1–RQ3 (RQ4 done in Phase F).

**Steps.**
1. **Saturation check (sample-size validity):** plot cumulative count of distinct leaves vs. number of AI samples; if still rising at k=15, log and recommend increasing k (Section 2.2). Include the curve in the appendix.
2. **Analysis set (Section 2.9).** Run the primary RQ1/RQ2 analysis on the **both-arm intersection** (problems with human **and** AI C++ non-AC ≥ 1); report the intersection problem count and per-arm total submissions. Problems present in only one arm are reported as secondary/descriptive only. The **primary** statistical model is problem-clustered (Section 2.7); unadjusted tests are shown for reference only.
3. **RQ1 — distributions.** Build per-arm leaf-frequency tables (and primary CE/RE/TLE/WA tables). Run chi-square/Fisher over the category vector; two-proportion tests per leaf with **BH-FDR** correction; report **Cramér's V** and per-leaf **Cohen's h**. **Sparse-category handling (Section 2.9 rule 7):** when a tested category's expected frequency is insufficient (rule of thumb < 5), collapse that leaf into its parent family or report it descriptively only. Lead hypothesis to test: "same categories, different frequencies" (CodeRabbit-style) vs. presence of arm-exclusive leaves (e.g., hallucinated API).
4. **RQ2 — distinctiveness under control.** Stratify by difficulty bin and algorithm type. Use CMH and/or logistic regression (`leaf_present ~ arm + difficulty + type`). Report adjusted odds/associations; identify AI-distinctive and human-distinctive leaves that survive controls.
5. **RQ3 — persistence.** Run the self-reflection residual set through the **same** Tiers 1–2. Compare the residual leaf distribution to the self-reflection arm's **own turn-0 (initial-attempt) distribution** — not the zero-shot corpus — to isolate what self-repair removes vs. leaves behind: which leaves are largely fixed by self-reflection (e.g., syntax) vs. which persist (expected: algorithmic/logic leaves). Present a per-leaf "fix rate across turns" table and a residual-distribution figure.
6. **Matched sensitivity analysis (Section 2.9 rule 8).** In addition to the main full-corpus + clustered analysis, produce an **appendix** 1:1 matched version: draw an equal random number of submissions per arm per problem (record the seed) and re-run RQ1, to preempt the "count imbalance is unfair" objection. Report whether conclusions hold.
7. **Generate every table/figure from scripts** into `results/tables/` and `results/figures/` (vector). No manual numbers.

**Deliverables.** `results/` fully populated; a `results/SUMMARY.md` with the key numbers per RQ.

## Appendix B — Verdict definitions
- **AC** — correct output for all tests within limits.
- **WA** — runs but wrong output on ≥1 test.
- **RE** — crashes (segfault, division-by-zero, uncaught exception, nonzero abnormal exit).
